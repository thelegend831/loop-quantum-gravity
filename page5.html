tetrad:
\( g_{ab} = {e_a}^I {e_b}^J \eta_{IJ} \)
\( \eta^{IJ} = {e_c}^I {e_d}^J g^{cd} \)

\( \eta_{IJ} = {e^a}_I {e^b}_J g_{ab} \)

orthonormality:
\( {e_a}^I {e^b}_I = \delta^b_a \)
\( {e^a}_I {e_a}^J = \delta^J_I \)
in coordinate frame: \( {e_a}^I = {{\partial x^I}\over{\partial x'^a}} \)

Notice that \( g_{ab} \) and \( g^{ab} \) are used to raise and lower curved spacetime coordinates.
Likewise \( \eta_{IJ} \) and \( \eta^{IJ} \) are used to raise and lower flat spacetime coordinates.
The vierbein \( {e_a}^I \) and inverse \( {e^a}_I \) are used to transform to and fro the curved coordinate metric and the Minkowski metric, and such transformations are expressed by changing the letter associated with the transform.
Notice that, if correct index applications are given, \( {e_a}^I \) is found to not equal \( {e^I}_a \) (but instead its transpose: http://physics.stackexchange.com/questions/142836/correct-tetrad-index-notation)
The convention I use for the vierbein tensors that transform between curved and Minkowski coordinates is to specify the curved coordinate first then the Minkowski second. Any other convention works, but you do need to distinguish your first and second indexes.

Also notice that, using our rule that indexes represent tetrad transformations, we find that:
\( \eta^{ab} = {e^a}_I \eta^{IJ} {e^b}_J = g^{ab} \)
\( \eta_{ab} = {e_a}^I \eta_{IJ} {e_b}^J = g_{ab} \)
\( e_{ab} = {e_a}^I e_{bI} = {e_a}^I \eta_{IJ} {e_b}^J = g_{ab} \)

So as long as you follow these rules.
(1) raising and lowering of curved spacetime coordinates is done by the metric tensor: \(g^{ab}\) and \(g_{ab}\), respectively.
(2) raising and lowering of Minkowski spacetime coordinates is done by the Minkowski metric tensor: \(\eta^{IJ}\) and \(\eta_{IJ}\), respectively.
(3) transforming from contravariant or covariant curved to Minkowski coordinates is done using \( {e_a}^I \) or \( {e^a}_I\), respectively.
...then all three tensors: \( g \), \(\eta\), and \(e\) could all be represented by a single symbol, with only their indexes necessary to determine which tensor is which.

Affine covariant and Lorentz covariant connection notation standards:
Affine connection: \( {\Gamma^a}_{bc} \) uses the first two terms to denote the linear transformation of the source tensor; and uses the third index to denote which derivative this is in regards to. Probably due to the old comma derivative convention when the derivative index was placed last.
Lorentz/spin connection: \( {\omega_{aI}}^J \) uses the last two indexes to denote the linear transformation of source tensor; and the first to denote which derivative this is in regards to. Probably due to the change in standards from using comma derivatives to writing prefix \( \partial \) symbols, where the derivative index would be seen first.
The two tensors are distinctly different, though some sources use the same symbol and rely on the use of coordinate vs Lorentz indexes to distinguish which tensor is used. This causes the representation of the connection transformed from one coordinate system to another to be ambiguous.
